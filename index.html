Q1
How would you grow OCBC's bancassurance penetration rate in Malaysia when competitors like Maybank and CIMB already dominate?
Suggested Answer
"We don't need to out-branch them. We need to out-integrate them."

Start by acknowledging the landscape honestly — Maybank owns Etiqa outright, CIMB has a long-term exclusive with Sun Life, and Public Bank partners with AIA. These are entrenched players with massive branch networks. OCBC Malaysia can't win a distribution volume war against Maybank's 2,400+ branches.

But here's OCBC's structural advantage that nobody else has: Great Eastern is not a partner — it's family. OCBC now holds ~94% of Great Eastern after the 2024 privatisation bid. This means tighter product co-development, faster speed-to-market, shared customer data (within regulatory limits), and aligned incentives that no commercial bancassurance arrangement can match. The Group CEO herself has said the strategy is to strengthen integration between banking units and Great Eastern for stronger product sales and share of wallet.

Prong 1: Go deep on the affluent segment, not wide on mass market. OCBC's Premier Banking and Premier Private Client segments are growing year-on-year. These customers have higher insurance needs — legacy planning, universal life, estate structuring. The average premium per policy is 5–10x mass market. Focus RM capacity here rather than competing head-to-head with Maybank on volume.

Prong 2: Plug bancassurance into existing banking moments. Mortgage approval? Bundle mortgage reducing term assurance. New Premier onboarding? Trigger a financial needs analysis that naturally surfaces protection gaps. The key is embedding insurance into banking workflows, not treating it as a separate sales push. Use OCBC's customer data to identify trigger events — salary increases, FD maturities, property purchases — and create automated RM prompts.

Prong 3: Build a takaful bancassurance line through OCBC Al-Amin. Malaysia is the global leader in Islamic finance and Family Takaful is the fastest-growing segment. OCBC already has an Islamic banking subsidiary. If Great Eastern can develop takaful-compliant products distributed through OCBC Al-Amin branches, that's an untapped channel most competitors haven't fully optimized.

Q2
If Great Eastern's products are underperforming versus AIA or Prudential, how would you still convince RMs to push them?
Suggested Answer
"I wouldn't try to convince RMs to push an inferior product. That destroys trust and leads to mis-selling. Instead, I'd take a three-step approach."

Step 1: Diagnose what "underperforming" actually means. Is it investment-linked fund returns? Claim settlement ratios? Premium competitiveness? Product feature gaps? Each has a different fix. If it's ILP fund performance, that's a Lion Global Investors conversation, not a product design issue. If it's pricing, I need to work with Great Eastern's actuarial team on cost structure.

Step 2: Reframe the value proposition beyond product returns. Great Eastern has an AA- rating from S&P, over S$100 billion in assets, and 16 million policyholders. It's the largest and oldest insurer in Singapore and Malaysia. The brand carries weight that AIA and Prudential don't in terms of group-level integration. Position it as: "When you sell Great Eastern, you're keeping the customer within the OCBC ecosystem — their banking, insurance, and investments all talk to each other. AIA can't offer that."

Step 3: Make it easier and more rewarding for RMs. Simplify the sales process — if Great Eastern's application takes 45 minutes and Prudential's takes 20, I've already lost. Work with Great Eastern to streamline e-submissions, reduce medical underwriting friction, and speed up policy issuance. On incentives, structure commission tiers that reward regular premium products (which build long-term relationships) over single-premium spikes. And create RM toolkits with side-by-side comparison sheets that honestly show where Great Eastern wins — because no product loses on everything.

"Ultimately, if the product genuinely can't compete, my job is to feed that intelligence back to Great Eastern's product team and co-develop something that can. That's the advantage of being in the same group."
Q3
What's your view on the future of bancassurance in Southeast Asia — is the tied-partnership model sustainable or should OCBC move to open architecture for insurance?
Suggested Answer
"The tied model is not just sustainable for OCBC — it's actually our competitive moat. But it needs to evolve."

The case for staying tied: OCBC's situation is fundamentally different from, say, a bank with a commercial bancassurance deal. OCBC owns Great Eastern. The integration allows for co-designed products, shared data insights, aligned incentive structures, and profit retention within the group. Great Eastern contributes 12-20% of OCBC Group's annual profit — that's real structural income you'd dilute by opening the shelf. DBS went exclusive with Manulife in a 15-year, USD 1.2 billion deal precisely because deep integration drives better outcomes than open architecture.

But here's where it needs to evolve: Customers are increasingly savvy. They compare products online. If OCBC only offers Great Eastern and it's clearly not the best option for a specific need, the customer goes elsewhere and you lose the banking relationship too.

My recommendation: Tied-first, open-selectively. Keep Great Eastern as the primary shelf for 90% of bancassurance needs. But for specific niches where Great Eastern has clear gaps — say, specialized high-net-worth products, or certain medical insurance riders — consider curating 1-2 third-party options. This is what Singapore already does with SGFinDex integration, allowing customers to view policies across 7 insurers on OCBC's platform.

"The strategic priority should be making Great Eastern's products so good that open architecture becomes irrelevant. That's what a Product Manager should be driving — not managing a multi-insurer shelf, but making sure the in-house shelf is best-in-class."
Q4
If you had to choose between launching a high-margin product with low customer demand vs. a low-margin product customers actually want, which do you pick and why?
Suggested Answer
"Short answer: I'd launch the product customers want — but I'd engineer the margin into it differently."

A high-margin product nobody wants is just an expensive line item on the product shelf. It consumes RM training time, marketing budget, and compliance resources but generates no volume. In bancassurance, where RM selling time is your scarcest resource, pushing a product with low demand means burning your most expensive distribution asset on low-conversion conversations. RMs lose confidence in the product, then in you as the product manager.

A low-margin product customers actually want does three critical things: it builds RM confidence because they can close deals, it brings customers into the OCBC-Great Eastern ecosystem where you can upsell over time, and it generates data on what customers value.

But I wouldn't accept low margin as a given. I'd look at how to restructure the economics — can we shift from single-premium to regular premium to improve lifetime value? Can we attach riders that increase the blended margin? Can we use the product as an anchor to cross-sell higher-margin wealth products? For example, a competitively priced term life product might be low-margin standalone, but if it brings in a Premier Banking customer who then takes up an endowment plan and a unit trust portfolio, the customer-level margin is excellent.

"The product that wins is the one that starts a relationship, not the one that maximizes a single transaction."
Q5
How would you use digital channels to sell insurance products without cannibalizing the RM relationship model?
Suggested Answer
"Digital should be the RM's best friend, not their replacement. The framework I'd use is: digital for awareness and simple products, RMs for advice and complex products."

Layer 1 — Digital self-serve for simple, low-touch products. Travel insurance, personal accident, basic term life with small sum assured. These don't need an RM conversation. Put them on the OCBC mobile app with a 3-click purchase flow. This actually frees up RM time for higher-value conversations. Most banks including OCBC already report year-on-year growth in online wealth product revenue in Singapore — extend this playbook to Malaysia.

Layer 2 — Digital as a lead generation engine for RMs. Use the app and online banking to surface insurance coverage gaps based on the customer's existing banking data. Customer just took a RM500k mortgage? The app shows them they have no mortgage protection and offers a "Talk to your RM" button. Customer's FD is maturing? Push a notification about endowment alternatives. The RM gets a warm lead with context, not a cold call. Digital does the prospecting; the RM does the advising.

Layer 3 — Digital tools that make the RM better, not redundant. Give RMs a tablet-based financial needs analysis tool that pulls in the customer's OCBC banking data in real-time. During a branch visit, the RM can show: "Here's your savings, here's your mortgage, here's your protection gap — and here's exactly how much coverage you need." This is consultative selling powered by digital, and it's what differentiates an OCBC RM from an AIA agent who doesn't have banking data.

The key metric to track: Digital should be increasing the RM's conversion rate and average case size, not replacing their transactions. If digital sells 1,000 travel insurance policies at RM50 each, and those customers are then flagged for RM follow-up on life insurance at RM5,000 each, everyone wins.

"The banks that treat digital as a competing channel will create internal conflict. The ones that treat it as a force multiplier for their RM network will win."

Weave in the data points naturally — Great Eastern ~94% ownership ~15% annual profit contribution ASEAN bancassurance ~6.5% CAGR Malaysia bancassurance ~45% of life insurance sales — it shows you've studied the company, not just the job description. And whenever possible, tie your answer back to the "One Group" integration story that OCBC's CEO has been pushing — that's the language the Head of WM will resonate with.

5 Qs
Q1
Walk me through how you'd take a new investment-linked insurance product from concept to launch — what are the critical gates?
Suggested Answer
Structure this as 6 gates. Be specific, not generic. She wants to hear that you've actually done this before or understand the real mechanics.

Gate 1 — Market Insight & Business Case (Weeks 1–4) Start with the "why." What customer need or market gap are we solving? I'd pull three data sources: internal data from OCBC's customer base (what's the protection gap among Premier customers? what asset classes are they underweight in?), competitive intelligence (what ILPs are AIA, Prudential, and Manulife selling well through bank channels right now?), and macro trends (interest rate direction, BNM regulatory outlook, equity market sentiment). From this, I build a one-page business case: target segment, projected annual premium equivalent, estimated margin, and how it fits into the existing wealth shelf. The critical question at this gate is: does this product fill a gap or just add noise?

Gate 2 — Product Design & Co-Development with Great Eastern (Weeks 5–10) This is where the OCBC-Great Eastern integration advantage kicks in. Unlike a commercial bancassurance deal where you're negotiating with an external insurer, I can sit with Great Eastern's actuarial and product teams directly. Key design decisions: what fund options to link to (Lion Global Investors funds give us another in-group advantage), sum assured structure, premium flexibility (single vs regular vs top-up), rider options (critical illness, waiver of premium), and the all-important surrender value schedule. I'd also design the product with the RM conversation in mind — if the product takes more than 15 minutes to explain, it's too complex. At this gate, I need sign-off from both OCBC wealth management and Great Eastern product leadership.

Gate 3 — Regulatory & Compliance (Weeks 8–14, overlapping with Gate 2) In Malaysia, this means BNM approval under the Financial Services Act. The product disclosure sheet, product highlights sheet, and policy contract all need legal and compliance review. For ILPs specifically, BNM is increasingly focused on ensuring customers understand investment risk — so the sales illustration methodology and benefit illustration need to be crystal clear. I'd also engage Shariah advisory if we're considering a takaful variant. The critical risk here is timeline — BNM approval can take 8–12 weeks, so I'd submit early and manage the parallel workstreams. This gate is a hard stop: no BNM approval, no launch.

Gate 4 — Distribution Readiness & RM Enablement (Weeks 12–16) This is the gate most product managers underestimate. The product is only as good as the RM's ability to sell it. I'd build: a 2-page product cheat sheet (not the 40-page product brochure), a 10-minute e-learning module, a needs-based selling script that ties the ILP to specific customer scenarios ("customer has RM200k in FD earning 3%, wants growth with protection"), and a competitive comparison card. Then I'd pilot with 10–15 top-performing RMs in selected branches for 2 weeks to test the sales conversation and get real feedback before national rollout. If RMs struggle to explain it in pilot, I go back and simplify before launching nationally.

Gate 5 — Systems, Operations & Launch (Weeks 14–18) E-application integration into the RM's sales platform, policy issuance workflow with Great Eastern, commission structure loaded into the system, marketing collateral produced, and customer-facing content on OCBC's website and app. I'd also set up the monitoring dashboard at this point — daily application submissions, conversion rates by branch, average premium size — so we're tracking from day one, not scrambling to build reporting after launch.

Gate 6 — Post-Launch Review at 30/60/90 Days Day 30: Are RMs submitting applications? If not, it's a distribution problem. Day 60: What's the conversion rate from needs analysis to application? If low, it's a product-market fit or pricing issue. Day 90: Full review — actual vs projected premium, customer profile vs target segment, persistency signals, and RM feedback. At 90 days, I make one of three calls: scale up, adjust, or deprioritize.

"The biggest lesson in product launches is that the product itself is maybe 40% of the success. The other 60% is distribution readiness and post-launch execution."
Q2
How do you prioritize which bancassurance products to push in a quarter when you have 15 products but limited RM bandwidth?
Suggested Answer
"RM selling time is the single most expensive and constrained resource in bancassurance. If I spread 15 products across the RM force, I get mediocrity across all of them. My framework narrows the focus to 3–4 hero products per quarter."

Step 1 — Score every product on a 2x2 matrix: Customer Demand vs. Strategic Value. Customer demand is driven by market conditions and customer needs right now. In a rising rate environment, endowment plans and participating policies with guaranteed components score high. In a bull market, ILPs score higher. Strategic value includes margin to the bank, persistency track record, cross-sell potential, and alignment with the Group's priorities (for example, if OCBC is pushing Premier Banking growth this year, products that attract or retain affluent customers score higher).

Step 2 — Apply the "RM minutes" test. Every product competes for the same RM conversation time. I'd estimate: how many minutes does it take to explain and close this product? What's the average premium? That gives me a "revenue per RM minute" metric. A universal life product might take 60 minutes but generate RM50,000 in premium. A personal accident plan takes 10 minutes but generates RM500. On a per-minute basis, the universal life wins — but the personal accident plan might have a 90% conversion rate vs 15%. So I need both metrics: revenue per minute AND conversion probability.

Step 3 — Select 3–4 hero products, assign to segments. For example in a given quarter: Product A (endowment plan) for mass affluent — high conversion, good margin, simple RM pitch. Product B (ILP with growth funds) for Premier Banking — higher ticket, aligns with wealth review conversations. Product C (mortgage reducing term) for new mortgage customers — trigger-based, almost automatic. Product D (universal life) for Premier Private Client — high premium, specialist BAS support needed.

Step 4 — Retire the noise. The remaining 11 products stay on the shelf for customer-initiated requests, but they get zero proactive marketing budget, zero training airtime, and zero contest points. This is the hard part — telling stakeholders their product isn't a priority this quarter. But if I don't do this, RMs default to selling whatever they personally understand best, which is usually the same 2 products regardless of customer needs.

Step 5 — Align incentives to the hero products. Contest points, commission accelerators, and branch-level scorecards should all weight toward the hero products. If the incentive structure still rewards all 15 equally, the prioritization exercise is meaningless.

"I'd review this every quarter and rotate based on market conditions, product lifecycle stage, and pipeline data. The worst thing a product manager can do is set priorities in January and never revisit them."
Q3
What KPIs would you set for a bancassurance product in its first 12 months, and what would make you kill it?
Suggested Answer
"I'd set KPIs across four categories, with different timelines for when I expect to see results."

Category 1 — Distribution Adoption (Month 1–3): Leading indicators RM participation rate: what percentage of RMs have submitted at least one application? Target should be 40–50% by month 3. If only 10% of RMs are selling it, I have a training or complexity problem, not a market problem. Also track: number of needs analyses conducted that include this product, and average time-to-first-sale per branch. These tell me whether the distribution engine is working before I can even judge the product itself.

Category 2 — Sales Performance (Month 1–12): Core metrics Annual Premium Equivalent (APE): the universal bancassurance metric. I'd set a monthly ramp — typically 60% of target by month 6, 100% by month 12 as RMs get comfortable. Average case size (premium per policy): tells me if we're reaching the right segment. Number of policies: volume metric, but less important than APE for high-value products. Single premium vs regular premium mix: I'd target at least 40% regular premium, because regular premium builds sustainable revenue and better persistency.

Category 3 — Quality & Persistency (Month 6–12): Health indicators 13-month persistency rate: this is the make-or-break metric. Industry average in Malaysia is around 80–85%. If my product falls below 75% by month 13, something is fundamentally wrong — either it's being mis-sold, or the product doesn't deliver what customers expected. Free-look cancellation rate: if more than 10–15% of customers cancel within the 15-day free-look period, that's an immediate red flag — RMs are probably not explaining the product properly. Complaint ratio: number of complaints per 1,000 policies sold.

Category 4 — Strategic Contribution (Month 6–12): Ecosystem metrics Cross-sell ratio: did customers who bought this product also increase their banking wallet with OCBC? New-to-bank ratio: what percentage of buyers are new OCBC customers vs existing? This tells me if the product is acquiring or just converting. Customer segment migration: are mass customers upgrading to Premier after buying higher-premium products?

What would make me kill it: I wouldn't kill a product based on any single metric. But I'd seriously consider it if three conditions are met simultaneously: APE is below 50% of target at month 9 (not just a slow start — sustained underperformance), 13-month persistency is below 70% (customers are leaving, meaning the product isn't delivering value), and RM participation has plateaued below 20% despite retraining and incentive adjustments (distribution has rejected the product).

Before killing it, I'd do a post-mortem: was it the product design, the pricing, the target segment, the RM training, or the competitive environment? Sometimes a product doesn't need killing — it needs repositioning to a different segment or repricing.

"Killing a product is a last resort. But keeping a zombie product on the shelf is worse — it wastes RM time, confuses customers, and drags down the brand."
Q4
How do you handle a situation where the insurance partner wants feature X but compliance says no?
Suggested Answer
"I've learned that 'compliance says no' is rarely the end of the conversation — it's usually the beginning of a better conversation. My approach has three steps."

Step 1 — Understand the actual regulatory concern, not just the "no." Compliance teams often say no because the request as phrased creates regulatory risk. But the underlying customer need might be perfectly solvable through a different mechanism. So I'd sit down with compliance — not over email, face-to-face — and ask: "What specifically about feature X creates risk? Is it the disclosure requirement? The suitability concern? The BNM guideline it potentially conflicts with?"

For example, Great Eastern might want to offer an auto-rebalancing feature on an ILP that shifts funds based on market conditions. Compliance might reject it because it looks like discretionary portfolio management, which requires a different licence. But if we reframe it as a customer-initiated model portfolio with annual opt-in confirmation, it might be compliant. Same customer benefit, different structure.

Step 2 — Bring both sides into the same room with a solution, not a complaint. The worst thing a product manager can do is shuttle between insurance and compliance like a messenger. I'd draft 2–3 alternative approaches that achieve the insurance team's commercial objective while staying within compliance boundaries, then facilitate a joint workshop. My job is to be the translator — Great Eastern thinks in product features and market competitiveness; compliance thinks in regulatory guidelines and risk exposure. I need to speak both languages.

Step 3 — Escalate with data, not emotion. If we genuinely reach an impasse where compliance is being overly conservative and it's costing us competitive positioning, I'd build a business case: "Here's what competitors are doing with this feature, here's the BNM guideline they're operating under, here's the revenue we're leaving on the table, and here's the specific risk we'd be taking." Then escalate to the Head of Wealth Management with a clear recommendation and the trade-offs laid out. I wouldn't ask them to override compliance. I'd ask them to make an informed risk-reward decision.

"The product manager who fights compliance becomes the problem. The product manager who partners with compliance to find creative solutions becomes indispensable. In my experience, 80% of compliance rejections can be resolved by redesigning the feature, not abandoning it."
Q5
What's your framework for pricing a bundled bancassurance product that sits between a pure investment and a pure protection plan?
Suggested Answer
"A bundled product — like a participating endowment or an ILP with insurance coverage — has pricing tension built in. The customer is paying for two things: a mortality charge (protection) and an investment allocation. My framework balances four dimensions."

Dimension 1 — Cost of Insurance (COI) vs. Investment Allocation Split Every ringgit of premium gets split between the insurance cost (mortality charges, expense loading, distribution cost) and the investment portion. The key decision is: what ratio feels fair to the customer while remaining profitable? For a product positioned more as "investment with protection," I'd target 70–80% allocation to the investment component in early years, ramping to 85–90% in later years. For a product positioned more as "protection with savings," the split might be 50/50 initially. The customer's perception of value hinges on this allocation ratio — and competitors publish theirs, so I need to be competitive.

Dimension 2 — Guaranteed vs. Non-Guaranteed Components This is where actuarial pricing meets customer expectation. The guaranteed component (e.g., guaranteed maturity value, guaranteed cash value) is expensive because the insurer bears the investment risk. The non-guaranteed component (bonuses, illustrated returns) is cheaper to price but harder for customers to trust. In the current environment, I'd push for a meaningful guaranteed floor — say, at least 100% of total premiums at maturity — because customers in Malaysia are risk-averse, especially post-pandemic. OCBC's MaxEmpower product already does this — guaranteeing 100% of premiums paid at maturity. That's the benchmark.

Dimension 3 — Distribution Cost & Commission Structure Bancassurance commission typically runs 30–50% of first-year premium for regular premium products and 3–5% for single premium. This cost is embedded in the pricing through the bid-offer spread (the difference between the price you buy units at and the price you sell them at) and the annual management charge. My job is to ensure the commission structure incentivizes the right RM behavior. If I overweight first-year commission, RMs push for single-premium sales and ignore persistency. If I build in trail commission (ongoing annual commission tied to policy persistency), RMs have an incentive to service the customer long-term. I'd aim for a blended structure: reasonable first-year commission to motivate acquisition, plus a trail commission from year 2 onwards that rewards retention.

Dimension 4 — Competitive Benchmarking & Price Sensitivity I'd map the product against three benchmarks: against pure investment alternatives (unit trusts, FDs) on net return after charges, against pure protection alternatives (term life) on cost per unit of coverage, and against direct competitors' bundled products on overall value. The product doesn't need to win all three comparisons — but it needs a clear story. For example: "You'll earn slightly less than a pure unit trust, but you get RM500k of life coverage included. You'll pay slightly more than term life, but you build cash value." The RM needs to be able to articulate this trade-off in one sentence.

How I'd pressure-test the pricing: Before launch, I'd run the pricing through three scenarios: base case (market returns at 5–6%), bull case (8–10%), and bear case (0–2%). In all three scenarios, the customer's outcome should make sense — they shouldn't feel cheated in a bear market, and the insurer shouldn't be giving away margin in a bull market. I'd also run a "competitor switch" test: if a customer cancels at year 3 and moves to a competitor product, do they lose so much on surrender value that it creates a complaint risk? Early surrender penalties that are too punitive create reputational damage.

"The best-priced product is one where the customer feels they got fair value, the RM feels confident recommending it, and the group retains healthy margin. If any one of those three stakeholders feels short-changed, the pricing is wrong."

Cross-Functional & Stakeholder Management
4 Qs
Q1
RMs are not selling your insurance products because commissions on unit trusts are higher — what do you do?
Suggested Answer
"I wouldn't blame the RMs. They're behaving rationally. If I can earn RM3,000 commission on a unit trust sale that takes 20 minutes, why would I spend 45 minutes on an insurance product that pays RM1,500? The problem isn't the RM — it's the system I've built around them."

Move 1 — Fix the commission comparison on a like-for-like basis. The dirty secret of unit trust vs insurance commission is that people compare upfront payouts without looking at total lifetime economics. A unit trust pays a one-time sales charge commission, and if the customer redeems in 6 months, that's it. An insurance regular premium product pays first-year commission PLUS trail commission for 5–10 years. So the total commission over the life of the policy often exceeds the unit trust. My first job is to make this visible to RMs. I'd build a simple "total earnings calculator" that shows: "If you sell one ILP at RM500/month regular premium, here's what you earn over 5 years vs a one-time RM50k unit trust sale." When RMs see the compounding trail income, the conversation shifts.

Move 2 — Don't compete with unit trusts. Bundle with them. Instead of positioning insurance as a substitute for unit trusts, position it as a complement. The wealth review conversation should be: "You have RM200k to deploy. Let's put RM120k in unit trusts for pure growth, and RM80k in an ILP that gives you both growth exposure AND RM500k life coverage your family needs." Now the RM isn't choosing between two products — they're doing a bigger total sale. My KPI for this would be "blended ticket size per customer interaction" rather than tracking insurance and unit trusts separately.

Move 3 — Restructure the scorecard so insurance isn't optional. This is the harder political conversation, but it's the real lever. If the RM's quarterly scorecard is 70% deposit and lending targets, 20% unit trust sales, and 10% insurance, they'll always treat insurance as the thing they do when they have spare time — which is never. I'd work with the Head of WM to rebalance the scorecard. My proposal: introduce a "wealth penetration ratio" that measures how many product categories each customer holds. An RM only gets full marks if their customers hold banking products AND investment AND insurance. This makes insurance a requirement for a good scorecard, not an optional bonus.

Move 4 — Reduce the effort gap. Even if commissions are equal, RMs will avoid insurance if the process is painful. Unit trust sales on OCBC's platform might take 15 minutes end-to-end. If an insurance application requires a separate paper form, medical declarations, 3-day underwriting, and back-and-forth with Great Eastern's operations team, I've lost before I started. I'd push aggressively for straight-through processing: digital application, real-time underwriting for simple products, and instant policy issuance for standard cases. The goal is to make selling insurance feel as frictionless as selling a unit trust.

"I'd never win by making unit trusts less attractive — that hurts the overall wealth business. I win by making insurance equally easy to sell, equally rewarding over time, and structurally required in the RM's scorecard."
Q2
How would you manage conflict between Great Eastern's product team and OCBC's wealth management priorities?
Suggested Answer
"This conflict is inevitable and actually healthy — it means both sides are advocating for their business. The problem is when it becomes personal or creates a deadlock that delays execution. My approach is to make the conflict productive."

First, understand why the conflict exists structurally. Great Eastern's product team is measured on insurance metrics: new business embedded value, total weighted new sales, persistency, claims ratios. OCBC's wealth management team is measured on banking metrics: AUM growth, wealth revenue, customer acquisition, cross-sell ratios. These objectives overlap but they're not identical. Great Eastern might want to push a high-premium universal life product because it maximizes their new business embedded value. OCBC's wealth team might want a simpler endowment plan that's easier for RMs to sell and attracts more customers into Premier Banking. Both are right from their own perspective.

My role as Product Manager is to be the bridge — not Great Eastern's advocate, not OCBC's advocate, but the customer's advocate. When I frame product decisions around "what does the target customer actually need and what will they buy," it depersonalizes the conflict. Instead of "Great Eastern wants X and OCBC wants Y," it becomes "our Premier Banking customer with RM300k in AUM and a young family needs Z — now let's design the best version of Z together."

Tactically, I'd establish three ground rules for the working relationship:

Rule 1 — Shared data, shared truth. A lot of conflict comes from each side having different data. Great Eastern sees its agency channel outperforming bancassurance and concludes OCBC's RMs aren't trying hard enough. OCBC sees low conversion rates and concludes Great Eastern's products aren't competitive. I'd establish a shared dashboard — joint visibility into application pipeline, conversion rates by product, RM feedback themes, and competitive benchmarking. When both sides look at the same data, the argument shifts from "whose fault is it" to "what do we fix."

Rule 2 — Joint quarterly product planning. Instead of Great Eastern developing products and throwing them over the wall to OCBC for distribution, I'd push for a joint planning cadence. Every quarter, both teams sit together and align on: which products are performing, which need adjustment, what's the competitive landscape, and what's the next launch priority. This prevents the classic scenario where Great Eastern spends 6 months developing a product that OCBC's RMs can't sell because nobody consulted them on the sales process.

Rule 3 — Escalation with recommendations, not complaints. When conflict does reach a deadlock — say, Great Eastern insists on a feature that adds complexity and OCBC's distribution team says RMs can't handle it — I'd escalate with a structured recommendation: here's what both sides want, here's the customer impact of each option, here's my recommendation, and here's what I need the senior leaders to decide. Never escalate with just the problem.

"The OCBC-Great Eastern relationship is actually a massive advantage over competitors with commercial bancassurance deals — Maybank owns Etiqa outright, so they've solved this by full integration. OCBC is heading the same direction with the ~94% ownership and potential delisting of Great Eastern. My job is to act as if we're already one team, even while the corporate structure is still catching up."
Q3
Tell me about a time you had to influence a senior stakeholder who fundamentally disagreed with your product direction.
Suggested Answer
Use the STAR-Plus format: Situation, Task, Action, Result — plus the lesson. Adapt the specifics to your own career.

Situation "In my previous role, I was leading the launch of [specific product — e.g., a regular premium investment-linked product / a new digital insurance offering / a revised product for the affluent segment]. The Head of [Sales/Distribution/a senior leader] fundamentally disagreed with the direction. Their position was [specific objection — e.g., 'our RMs aren't ready for this complexity,' or 'the market timing is wrong,' or 'we should focus on single premium products because they hit revenue targets faster']."

Task "I needed this person's support because without their buy-in, the distribution team wouldn't prioritize the product. I couldn't go around them — they controlled the RM force."

Action — Three specific influence moves:

"First, I listened before I pitched." I asked for a 30-minute meeting just to understand their concerns — not to persuade, just to listen. What I discovered was that their objection wasn't really about the product itself. An ILP bridges Needs 2 and 3 — investment growth with life protection. A universal life plan bridges Needs 3 and 4 — protection with legacy planning. A protection-first approach generates better fact-finding scores (because you're doing genuine needs analysis) and better persistency scores (because protection products stick). 5 Qs
Q1
Most RMs are not insurance experts — how do you make bancassurance simple enough for them to sell confidently in a 30-minute meeting?
Suggested Answer
"I'd start with a mindset shift: I don't need RMs to become insurance experts. I need them to become financial needs detectors. The expertise sits with the product and the Bancassurance Specialist — the RM's job is to identify the need and open the door."

Simplification Layer 1 — Reduce the conversation from product explanation to need identification. The reason insurance takes 45 minutes is because RMs try to explain product mechanics: fund allocation rates, mortality charges, surrender value schedules. Customers don't care. They care about one question: "What happens to my family if something happens to me?"

I'd redesign the RM conversation around three questions, not product features:

Question 1: "If something happened to you tomorrow, would your family have enough to maintain their lifestyle for the next 10 years?" This opens the life protection conversation. Simple formula: monthly expenses × 12 × 10 years, minus existing coverage and savings. If there's a gap, there's a need.

Question 2: "If you were diagnosed with a critical illness today, could you cover RM200k–500k in treatment costs without touching your savings?" This opens the health protection conversation.

Question 3: "Are you saving enough to reach your retirement/education/legacy goal, and what happens to that plan if your income stops?" This opens the accumulation-with-protection conversation — where ILPs and endowments live.

Three questions, 10 minutes. The RM hasn't mentioned a single product yet. But they've identified whether the customer has a protection gap, a health coverage gap, or an accumulation gap.

Simplification Layer 2 — One-page product cheat sheets, not 40-page brochures. For each hero product, a single A4 sheet with five sections: who is this product for (one sentence), what problem it solves (one sentence), how it works in plain language (three bullet points max), key numbers (premium range, coverage amount, guaranteed maturity value), and the one killer line that closes the deal — a pre-written sentence the RM can use verbatim, such as "For RM300 a month — less than your phone bill — your family would receive RM500k if anything happened to you."

The tool instantly outputs: protection gap in ringgit, recommended coverage amount, and top 2 product recommendations with indicative premiums. The RM shows the customer the screen: "Based on what you've told me, you have a RM800k protection gap. Here are two ways to close it — one at RM250/month, one at RM450/month with investment growth included." The RM didn't need insurance expertise. The tool did the analysis. The RM facilitated the conversation.

Simplification Layer 4 — The Bancassurance Specialist as the safety net. For complex cases — universal life, high-sum-assured ILPs, medical insurance with underwriting complications — the handoff protocol should be seamless: RM identifies the need using the three questions, books a joint meeting with the BAS within 48 hours, BAS handles the product deep-dive and closes the sale, RM stays in the meeting to maintain the relationship. This means the RM only needs to be competent at need identification (a 30-minute skill to teach), not at product structuring (a 30-month skill to build).

"The metric I'd track isn't 'how well do RMs understand insurance products' — it's 'how confidently do RMs initiate insurance conversations.' Suggested Answer

A needs-based approach produces sales across different age groups, income levels, and family stages. I'd flag any RM whose customer profile is suspiciously homogeneous.

Any RM above 10% free-look cancellation gets immediate coaching. Above 15% gets a compliance review. 13-month persistency below 75% for a specific RM means their customers' needs weren't properly matched.

Wealth Portfolio & Asset Allocation Thinking
5 Qs
Q1
A customer's portfolio is 80% equities — what bancassurance product would you recommend and how do you frame it in asset allocation language?
Suggested Answer
"An 80% equity portfolio tells me three things: high risk tolerance, growth-oriented with a long time horizon, and dangerously concentrated. My first job isn't to sell them insurance — it's to show them the risk they can't see."

The portfolio problem in asset allocation language: "You've built a strong growth engine. But right now, 80% of your wealth moves in the same direction as the stock market. In 2022, the MSCI Asia Pacific index dropped roughly 20%. If your portfolio mirrors that, you'd have lost RM160k on an RM800k equity portfolio in a single year. You have almost no allocation to non-correlated or guaranteed-return assets. And more importantly — if something happens to you during a market downturn, your family would inherit a portfolio that's down 20–30%, which they may be forced to liquidate at the worst possible time."

That last sentence is the bridge from asset allocation to insurance.

Layer 1: Term life or whole life with high sum assured (pure protection allocation). Frame it as: "Think of this as the hedge on your portfolio. If markets crash 30% and something happens to you simultaneously, this policy delivers a guaranteed RM1–2M to your family regardless of what the KLCI is doing. It's the one asset with zero market correlation. In portfolio language, this is your tail-risk insurance — it costs less than 1% of your portfolio value annually, the same as what institutional investors pay for portfolio hedging through options."

Layer 2: Participating endowment or guaranteed-return product (fixed income substitute). Frame it as: "Your portfolio has almost no fixed income exposure. Rather than buying bonds directly, consider a participating endowment — it provides guaranteed maturity value (your principal protection) plus non-guaranteed bonuses that have historically delivered 4–5% over the long term. It behaves like a bond allocation with upside potential, and comes with life coverage built in." Allocate 10–15% of total wealth here. The portfolio goes from 80/20 equities/cash to roughly 65/15/10/10 — equities/endowment/cash/term life coverage. That's a dramatically more resilient portfolio.

Layer 3 (if applicable): ILP with conservative or balanced fund option (diversified growth). If the customer insists on high equity exposure, an ILP allocated to a balanced fund (60/40 equity-bond mix) provides diversification the standalone portfolio lacks — plus insurance coverage. Frame it as: "This gives you diversified growth with automatic rebalancing, and the insurance component means a portion of your wealth is protected regardless of market direction."

The pitch in one sentence: "Right now your portfolio is an accelerator with no brakes. I'm not asking you to slow down — I'm asking you to install a braking system. The term life is your emergency brake. The endowment is your cruise control. Together, they let you keep 65% in equities with confidence because the downside is covered."

"I'd never tell a growth-oriented customer to stop investing in equities. But the smartest institutional investors in the world — pension funds, endowments, sovereign wealth funds — all allocate 15–25% to non-correlated and guaranteed assets. I'm just helping them do the same at the personal portfolio level, with insurance as the instrument."
Q2

Suggested Answer

Q3
Should bancassurance eventually sit under wealth management, under partnerships, or as its own P&L — and why does it matter?
Suggested Answer
"My answer is unequivocal: bancassurance should sit under wealth management. Not under partnerships, not as its own P&L. And the reason is both strategic and practical."

Why not under partnerships? The partnerships model makes sense when bancassurance is a commercial distribution agreement with an external insurer — like CIMB-Sun Life. The skill set is relationship management and contract governance. But OCBC doesn't have a commercial partnership — OCBC owns Great Eastern at ~94%. Putting bancassurance under partnerships signals to the organization that insurance is "someone else's product that we happen to sell" rather than "a core capability of our wealth franchise." That signal matters enormously for how RMs, branch managers, and product teams prioritize insurance daily.

Why not as its own P&L? A standalone P&L creates accountability (good) but also creates silos (fatal). Three things happen: First, the bancassurance team optimizes for insurance sales independent of the customer's overall wealth needs — product-pushing replaces needs-based advice. Second, bancassurance competes with wealth management for RM time instead of collaborating — two internal teams fight over the same RM's calendar. The RM is caught in the middle receiving conflicting directions. Third, integrated product design becomes nearly impossible — every joint initiative requires cross-P&L negotiation on revenue credit, cost bearing, and customer ownership. These negotiations slow innovation and create political friction unrelated to customer value.

Why under wealth management — the strategic reason: Insurance IS wealth management. Protection ensures the wealth strategy survives a life event. Endowments and ILPs are wealth instruments with insurance wrappers. Universal life is a wealth transfer mechanism. This aligns with OCBC Group's stated strategy — the Group CEO has repeatedly framed OCBC-Great Eastern integration as a wealth management play offering customers comprehensive "banking, wealth, health and protection products." The organizational structure should reflect this strategic intent.

The practical reason — aligned incentives: When the Head of WM owns all wealth revenue — investments, insurance, structured products, DPM — they can optimize the total mix. If shifting 20% of RM time from unit trusts to insurance increases total wealth revenue, the Head of WM makes that call because both revenue streams sit in their P&L. If bancassurance were separate, that negotiation would require two P&L owners to agree — which often means it doesn't happen. The unified P&L also enables consistent scorecards and the "products per customer" metric that rewards RMs for selling across the full shelf.

The practical reason — infrastructure access: Under wealth management, bancassurance benefits from the existing Premier Banking relationship model, PPC advisory framework, CRM platform, wealth review process, and RM training infrastructure. These are expensive capabilities already funded by the wealth management budget. A standalone P&L would either duplicate them (wasteful) or negotiate access (bureaucratic).

How I'd structure it specifically: Bancassurance product management (my role) reports directly to the Head of Wealth Management. BAS team reports to me functionally, deployed to branches under branch manager operational oversight. Product development is a joint workstream with Great Eastern, funded by a shared innovation budget. Revenue shows up as a distinct line item within the wealth management P&L — visibility is maintained, but the P&L owner is the Head of WM, ensuring integrated decision-making.

"The bank that treats insurance as a partnership manages a contract. The bank that treats insurance as a separate P&L manages a product line. But the bank that treats insurance as part of wealth management manages a customer relationship. And in the long run, the bank that manages the customer relationship wins everything else — the deposits, the investments, the insurance, and the loyalty. That's why bancassurance belongs under wealth management, and that's why I want to build it here."
